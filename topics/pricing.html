    Pricing Strategy

    Willingness-to-pay, elasticity, and capturing value.

    Three pricing logics

    Firms set prices using one of three logics: cost-plus, competition-based, or value-based 1. Cost-plus is operationally simple but ignores the customer; competition-based anchors on rivals and races to the floor; value-based pricing starts from the customer's willingness-to-pay (WTP) and captures the largest share of created value.

    Willingness-to-pay (WTP) The maximum price at which a specific customer will still buy. Aggregating WTP across customers gives the demand curve.

    The value stick

    A simple mental model from Felix Oberholzer-Gee: value created = WTP − cost. Price splits that value between customer surplus (WTP − price) and firm margin (price − cost) 2.

    Price elasticity of demand

    Elasticity ε = (% Δ quantity) / (% Δ price). When |ε| > 1 demand is elastic — price cuts raise revenue. When |ε| < 1 demand is inelastic — price hikes raise revenue3.

    Optimal markup rule For a monopolist: (P − MC) / P = 1 / |ε|. The less elastic the demand, the higher the optimal margin.

    Drivers of elasticity

    • Availability of substitutes (more substitutes → more elastic).
    • Share of wallet (big-ticket items → more elastic).
    • Urgency / necessity (urgent or essential → less elastic).
    • Switching costs (high lock-in → less elastic).

    Price discrimination

    Charging different prices to different customers for (nearly) the same product captures more of the WTP curve .

    DegreeMechanismExample
    1stPersonalized price per customerNegotiated B2B, dynamic pricing
    2ndSelf-selection via versionsEconomy / business class, tiers
    3rdGroup pricingStudent / senior discounts

    Behavioral pricing

    Buyers are not rational utility maximizers. Anchoring sets a reference point that pulls perceived value 4. The decoy effect: adding a clearly inferior option boosts the target option's chosen rate. Charm pricing ($9.99) and price framing ("$1/day" vs "$365/year") meaningfully shift demand.

    Dynamic and freemium models

    Dynamic pricing (airlines, ride-share, e-commerce) treats price as a real-time signal that clears the market. Freemium gives away a baseline product to harvest data and convert a small percentage to paid tiers; it works only when the marginal cost of a free user is near zero and there is a meaningful upgrade trigger.

    "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get." — Warren Buffett

    Margin notes

    1. Surveys consistently find cost-plus is the most-used method in practice yet the most-criticized in theory. Path of least internal resistance.
    2. Symmetrically at the bottom: cost − supplier WTS = supplier surplus. Pushing supplier WTS down (better terms, lower friction) creates value too.
    3. Most firms underestimate their own elasticity because they only observe one price point. A/B price tests reveal the true curve.
    4. Classic Ariely experiment: Economist subscription bundle. Adding a "print only" decoy at the same price as "print + web" tripled the high-tier choice rate.
