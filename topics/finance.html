    Corporate Finance

    The time value of money, valuation, and capital structure.

    Time value of money

    A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow because today's dollar can be invested at some rate r1. The two foundational operations are compounding (future value) and discounting (present value).

    Present Value (PV) PV = CF / (1 + r)t. The value today of a cash flow CF received at time t, discounted at rate r.

    Net Present Value (NPV)

    NPV = Σ CFt / (1 + r)t. The NPV rule says: accept any project with positive NPV; among mutually exclusive projects, take the one with the highest NPV2.

    Why not IRR?

    IRR (internal rate of return) gives the discount rate that makes NPV = 0. It is intuitive but breaks down with non-conventional cash flows and mis-ranks mutually exclusive projects of different scales.

    Capital structure

    A firm finances assets with debt and equity. The Modigliani–Miller theorem (in a frictionless world) says capital structure is irrelevant to firm value. Real-world frictions — taxes, bankruptcy costs, agency costs — make the choice matter3.

    WACC

    Weighted Average Cost of Capital WACC = (E/V) · rE + (D/V) · rD · (1 − Tc). The blended after-tax discount rate a firm should use for typical-risk projects.

    CAPM and the cost of equity

    The Capital Asset Pricing Model prices the cost of equity as rE = rf + β · (rm − rf), where β measures sensitivity to market risk.

    Margin notes

    1. r reflects opportunity cost + risk + inflation. Choosing r is the hard part of any DCF.
    2. NPV beats payback period and ARR because only NPV consistently maximizes shareholder value.
    3. Trade-off theory: tax shield from debt vs. expected bankruptcy costs. Pecking-order theory: managers prefer internal funds → debt → equity.
