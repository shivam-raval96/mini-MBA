    Strategy

    Competitive positioning, industry analysis, and value creation.

    What is strategy?

    Strategy is the set of integrated choices about where to play and how to win1. It is not a plan or a goal — it is a positioning of a firm relative to its industry and rivals such that it can sustain above-average returns over time.

    Strategy An integrated set of choices about where to compete and how to win, designed to create a sustainable competitive advantage.

    A useful distinction: operational effectiveness means doing the same activities better than rivals. Strategic positioning means performing different activities, or the same activities in different ways 2.

    Porter's Five Forces

    Industry profitability is shaped by five competitive forces identified by Porter. The stronger each force, the more profits are dissipated to suppliers, buyers, substitutes, entrants, or rivals.

    Threat of new entrants

    Determined by barriers to entry: economies of scale, capital requirements, switching costs, network effects, and incumbent retaliation. High barriers keep entrant threat low3.

    Bargaining power of suppliers and buyers

    Concentrated suppliers with differentiated inputs and few substitutes can extract value. Symmetrically, concentrated buyers facing undifferentiated products can demand price cuts.

    Threat of substitutes

    Substitutes cap the price the industry can charge. Note: substitutes are from other industries that meet the same need (e.g., video calls substituting for airline travel).

    Rivalry

    Intensified by many equally-balanced competitors, slow industry growth, high fixed costs, and lack of differentiation.

    Value chain

    A firm's value chain decomposes its activities into primary (inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing & sales, service) and support (firm infrastructure, HR, technology, procurement) activities. Competitive advantage arises from doing these activities at lower cost or in a more differentiated way than rivals.

    Tests for a good strategy

    Rumelt proposes that good strategy has a kernel: (i) a diagnosis, (ii) a guiding policy, and (iii) coherent actions4. Bad strategy is fluff, failure to face the challenge, mistaking goals for strategy, and bad strategic objectives.

    "The core of strategy work is always the same: discovering the critical factors in a situation and designing a way of coordinating and focusing actions to deal with those factors."

    Margin notes

    1. Lafley & Martin's framing in Playing to Win: strategy = an integrated cascade of five choices, anchored on "where to play / how to win."
    2. Operational effectiveness is necessary but not sufficient. Many rivals can copy best practices; few can copy a coherent system of activities.
    3. Note that in digital markets, network effects and data accumulation are increasingly dominant barriers, often more so than capital.
    4. Rumelt's diagnosis stage is often the hardest — leaders skip to action before genuinely understanding the obstacle.
